# Storytelling Architect

## Purpose

Create practical brand, product, founder, campaign, and market-entry stories that make complex value clear, memorable, credible, and emotionally relevant.

This skill helps turn facts, proof, history, customer problems, product technology, and business milestones into structured stories for websites, pitches, PR, social content, B2B decks, and marketplace brand content.

## When to Use

Use this skill when the task is to:

- create a brand story;
- explain a product story;
- build founder or company narrative;
- make technical product benefits easier to understand;
- prepare PR or pitch storytelling;
- create campaign narrative;
- improve website or sales storytelling.

## When NOT to Use

Do not use when the user only needs a short slogan, one ad, or a technical product specification. Use Copywriting or Product Marketing skills for final execution after the story structure is clear.

## Inputs

Useful inputs:

- brand or product;
- audience;
- customer problem;
- product difference;
- proof points;
- founder or company background;
- market context;
- tone;
- channel;
- desired action.

## Workflow

1. Define the audience and desired belief shift.
2. Identify the core conflict or problem.
3. Clarify the transformation the brand or product enables.
4. Select the story type: origin, customer, product, mission, proof, campaign, or market entry.
5. Build the narrative arc.
6. Add proof and specific details.
7. Adapt the story to the channel.
8. Create concise message variants.

## Output

Always provide:

- story goal;
- audience;
- core conflict;
- transformation;
- narrative arc;
- proof points;
- channel adaptation;
- short version;
- long version outline;
- next steps.

## Best Practices

- Make the customer the center of the story.
- Use concrete details instead of generic inspiration.
- Connect emotion with proof.
- Keep one clear transformation.
- Adapt the same story to each channel.

## Common Mistakes

- Telling company history without customer relevance.
- Using vague inspirational language.
- Adding too many messages into one story.
- Forgetting proof.
- Making the story too long for the channel.
